When I took my first economics course, ECON 101, I thought the discipline was mostly about 
graphs, supply and demand curves, and abstract market theories. It wasn't until 
I spent a summer sourcing at Amazon that I realized economics is everywhere — including in the way companies hire people.

## Labor Markets Are Real

Textbooks describe labor markets as systems where wages adjust until supply meets 
demand. In practice, I saw this play out every day. Roles in Amazon's emerging 
Automotive division were hard to fill not because candidates didn't exist, but 
because the market for that specific skill set was incredibly limited. The supply 
was low, the demand was high, and the competition between firms was intense.

## Information Asymmetry Is a Real Problem

One of the most interesting concepts in economics is information asymmetry — the 
idea that one party in a transaction knows more than the other. In recruiting, 
this exists everywhere. Candidates don't know exactly what a company values, and 
companies don't know exactly what a candidate is capable of. A huge part of my 
job was essentially bridging that gap by crafting outreach that communicated value 
clearly on both sides.

## Incentives Drive Everything

Whether it was motivating candidates to respond to a cold message or aligning 
recruitment targets with business goals, everything came down to incentives. 
Understanding what people want — and what they're afraid of — is just applied 
economics.

My summer at Amazon gave me a new lens for seeing how the theories I study in class play out in the real world. And honestly, that made going back to school a lot more interesting.
